Overview

As part of the seminars I attended and also during my training as a non-medical practitioner for psychotherapy, I came into practical contact with a few selected psychological counseling tools, including:

  • Resource-oriented advice
  • Client-centered counseling according to Carl Rogers
  • Solution-oriented short-term therapy according to Steve de Shazer
  • Systemic issues and perspective
  • Definition of goals
  • Nonviolent communication according to Rosenberg
  • Progressive muscle relaxation according to Jacobson
  • Mindfulness and breathing techniques

There are a variety of approaches to support people psychologically. I do not want to give a comprehensive overview at this point. With friendly approval I refer you to: https://www.therapie.de/psyche/info/therapie/einleitung/ or https://thehumancondition.com/psychotherapy-introduction-benefits/

Of all the existing viewpoints, I prefer mindfulness-based and humanistic approaches (client-centered counseling: https://www.therapie.de/psyche/info/therapie/gespraechspsychotherapie/, https://www.psychologytoday.com/intl/therapy-types/person-centered-therapy ). They form the core of my consultations.

I have the attitude that every client already knows the solution to their problem within themselves and takes this knowing in the consultation. My role is to encourage the emergence of what Rogers calls „actualizing tendency“. I do this by carefully reflecting on what you bring to the counseling session. The core principles of my work are discretion, authenticity, empathy and appreciation, no matter where you stand. An encounter at eye level is essential for me - from person to person.

The Body, the Breath and Awareness

For a couple of years my own breath increasingly draws my attention. There is much usefulness in looking more carefully at this phenomenon...

The breath has long been seen as a link between the material world, i.e. the human body and the mind / psyche. This is reflected in substitute words from different languages:

  • Old Indian prana → bodily breath, air | essence of life;
  • Chinese Chi → natural air | life energy;
  • Greek pneuma → air, breath | spirit, life essence;
  • Greek phren → diaphragm | Spirit;
  • Hawaiian ha (in aloha) → spirit | wind, air | Breath;
  • Jewish ruach → breath | creative spirit;
  • Latin spiritus → breath | Spirit

Likewise in the Slavic languages, spirit and breath have the same linguistic roots.

The psychotherapist Ernst Andreas Stadter writes: "Is it flat, heavy, light, calm, rushed? The breath says a lot, basically everything about my current state of mind. That is an ancient wisdom of many peoples and cultures."

The psychiatrist Wilhelm Reich ( † 1957) has in this context made an interesting observation: he thought he had recognized that psychological resistance and defense mechanisms are related to restricted breathing. This was confirmed by Stanislav Grof, a Czech psychiatrist.

Mijares and Bessel van der Kolk also write about this analogously: "By observing the rhythm of your breathing, you can see your physical and emotional state, and you can change that state by changing your breath...".

In our culture, Wilhelm Reich was one of the first to include the body and breathing in psychotherapy.

His main thesis is that the body accumulates and holds tension in the muscles, and occasionally at the same time restricts breathing, to unconsciously protect us from unwelcome emotions.

Reich observed that many people, partly unconsciously, have a very limited breathing pattern. A shallow breath, for example, about ten times a minute into the upper part of the lungs is widespread.

In body psychotherapy a limitation of self-expression is seen as an unconscious purpose of such limited breathing. Something must not or should not be shown or felt. Full perception and feeling is restricted.

Below is a small bioenergetic exercise (bioenergetic therapy = a form of body psychotherapy). Anyone interested is welcome to try it out.

  1. Place your hand on your chest and breathe in and out naturally. Notice how your chest feels.
  2. Remove your hand from your chest and place your little finger on your belly button while resting the rest of your hand on the abdominal area above the belly button below your chest. Breathe in and out, noticing how that abdominal area feels under your hand.
  3. Place your thumb on your belly button and rest your hand on the abdominal area below. Breathe in and breathe out. Feel this area too.
  4. Focus your attention on the breath and notice which of the three areas described above moves the least during the inhalation. The area of least movement indicates an area of "blocked energy." Place your hand on the area that is most restricted and increase or deepen your breathing.
  5. If this is difficult, it CAN be a sign of chronic tension in this area.

Breathing is a bodily process that can generally take place consciously or unconsciously. The conscious modification of the breath is used in many spiritual practices, such as the ancient Indian science of breath pranayama, Kundalini Yoga, Siddha Yoga, Burmese Buddhist meditation, or Sufi practice.

I have been practicing Vipassana Meditation as taught by S.N. Goenka for some years. The first step in this form of mindfulness meditation is the conscious observation of your own breath (Anapanasati; from the Pali anapana=breath in and out and sati=mindfulness, literally "mindfulness of breathing"). It is not changed, but left naturally, as it is. If it is fast, then it is fast, if it is heavy, then it is heavy...

Similarly, the described body psychotherapies also include the conscious observation of the natural breathing process. Compared to the above spiritual practices, the modification of the breath at will is NOT part of anapanasati.

Essentially two abilities are trained: on the one hand, the "faculty of awareness" (latin facultas "ability"). On the other hand, it is to be practiced not to react mentally to what is perceived (exercise of equanimity = equanimity ≠ indifference).

The body position is an upright sitting, as comfortably as possible (e.g. on a chair). The back should be straight and the rest of the body relaxed. The eyes remain closed. As soon as a distraction occurs in the form of a feeling or thought - e.g. after five minutes you realize that you were "somewhere else" - you return to the breath.

Thoughts are not pursued during practice, they are allowed to come and go, arise and die away, without being attached to them. Again: Thoughts are allowed to arise and be there, you just shouldn't cling to them, "follow" them. From another perspective, it is about intentionally bringing attention to the present moment without evaluating what is showing up in the present moment. If ratings and judgments occur, it is important to become aware of them and not "get caught up" in them, e.g. by mentally "sticking to them" (okay, enough metaphors :-).

The focus remains on the (natural) breath throughout. The exit of the nostrils, for example, can be used as an aid. One can observe the flow of air and the sensations there on the inner walls of the nose. "When I breathe in, I'm aware that I'm breathing in. When I breathe out, I am aware that I am breathing out.” This is a sentence that has often helped me during practicing.

In a second step of Vipassana, attention is expanded, and the sensations throughout the body are systematically focused (body scan). Here, too, it is about consciously perceiving without reacting mentally.

The systematic practice of mindfulness (methods) is part of many counseling and therapeutic approaches (e.g. MBSR or Acceptance and Commitment Therapy from behavioral therapy). It is an essential part of my work.

It is in the nature of things that we have a tendency to seek fulfillment in the "outside", in the material world. Buddhism emphasizes that this can only be sought (and found?) “inside”. The power of the breath entails counteracting this inherent tendency in us. The practice of keeping your attention on the natural breath calms the mind. I find two analogies very apt here: the mind/mental state as water e.g. a calm forest pond under a full moon.

When everything is calm, even the rarest animals come out of their hiding places to the water. Another metaphor is a cup with dirty water swirling around in it. In the course of the meditation, the liquid clears and the dirt (= thoughts) settle:

After a little practice, the result is most often more serenity, stability and clarity. Qualities that strengthen with regular practice and also show up outside of formal meditation. Irritability and nervousness and a calm state of mind are mutually exclusive. The latter is often the prerequisite for any successful psychological intervention/measure.

To round off, I want to clarify one more thing: the practice of meditation is something I have experience with. It helped me a lot. That is not to say that this is the appropriate method of choice for everyone. There is an uncalculable number of tools and methods to achieve more clarity and minimize mental suffering. It is up to you to decide which approach is the right one for you.
